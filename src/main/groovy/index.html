Apathy towards the Environment as a Social Problem

Thesis

I’m in the Social Problems class at Penn State Mont Alto taught by Larry Murray. My topic for the final project is the indifference of society towards the environment. In other words, if someone sees trash laying on the ground that isn’t theirs, or in some cases even if it is their trash, he will just walk right by without bothering to pick it up. The same thing can be said for factories that dump their waste into the environment.

I decided that this would be my topic because I’m very much like this and also because of an experience that inspired me. A while back, I saw a water bottle sitting on the stairs of Mont Alto Hall and nobody, including me, bothered to pick it up. Recollecting on it, I knew it would be my topic because little amounts of trash like that add up to create landfills of pollution.

My experiment that I’m going to conduct is as follows. I’m going to set a water bottle on the stairs leading up to Mont Alto Hall (a place that many people walk by where I can set up surveillance) and see how many people bother to pick it up. Background on pollution

To start, I want to give a definition of pollution in two different ways; first, the formal definition, and after, a process definition. The formal definition of pollution of the environment is best defined as the process of "contamination of air, water, or soil by substances that are harmful to living organisms. Pollution can occur naturally, for example through volcanic eruptions, or as the result of human activities, such as the spilling of oil or disposal of industrial waste." For the process definition: all processes in the world require some form of input and generate a form of output that is recycled and eliminated by the environment. An example of this is photosynthesis. Plants use carbon dioxide, water, and sunlight to make oxygen and sugar. The input: carbon dioxide, water, and sunlight; the output: oxygen and sugar. The oxygen is then recycled by the creatures, eliminating the by-product that resulted from the original process.

Another example process of this is pollution. Pollution can, if given time, be decomposed by the environment, which would result in all human by-product being disposed of. So you might be asking, "Then why is pollution such a problem?" The reason is because pollution is spiking, meaning that there are such high rates of pollution that the environment cannot break the trash down before the next flow of trash comes, which creates an overflow. Reasons for this overflow will be talked about in Links.
